***Abstract***

*Misinformation is used by institutions and decision-makers on a daily basis to
advance their own political and economic interests. To better understand the
impact of these influences, we examined the effect of different types of
retractions on the Continued Influence Effect. Using a newspaper article, we
compared the the influence of a retraction accompanied by the presentation of
an uncertain alternative piece of information with a retraction without and
with a certain alternative piece of information. In a between-subjects design
with a sample of 753 participants, we were able to replicate that a certain
alternative piece of information reduces the Continued Influence Effect.
Further comparisons of the experimental conditions yielded no significant
results. Furthermore, fact recall performance was very similar across the
experimental conditions. Among participants who believed the correction, we
found a weak correlation indicating that the less participants believe the
correction of the misinformation, the more inferences they draw from it. One
possible interpretation of the results is based on Johnson and Laird’s theory
of mental models. Future research could examine realistic user interfaces, such
as online forums and news services. This could help investigate the influence
of the medium on the Continued Influence Effect and clarify whether
misinformation spreads more easily online.*



Over the past few decades, media culture around the world has changed due to
the rise of the internet and the transition into the information age. Today,
the media is often misused by individuals and institutions to advance their own
agendas and polarize public opinion. In the process, misleading, inaccurate, or
false information is disseminated, which often has negative societal
consequences.  A well-known example is former U.S. President Donald Trump, who
during his four-year term spread more than 30,000 false or misleading claims,
many of them hundreds of times ([Glenn Kessler et al.,
2021](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2021/01/24/trumps-false-or-misleading-claims-total-30573-over-four-years/)).
Through him and others, more than half of all misinformation regarding COVID-19
was spread via social media half of all misinformation regarding COVID-19
([Naeem et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1111/hir.12320)  ). This dynamic led
to reduced willingness to get vaccinated, the spread of unproven treatment
methods, and ultimately hindered the achievement of herd immunity ([Borges do
Nascimento et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.2471/BLT.21.287654)).  Research
into the effects of misinformation has its origins in a pioneering experiment
by [Johnson and Seifert (1994)](https://doi.org/10.1037/0278- 7393.20.6.1420).
They found that misinformation continues to influence conclusions formed later,
even if it was corrected before the inference was made. They termed the
phenomenon of continuing to trust misinformation even after recalling its
correction the Continued Influence Effect.  Replications of the effect have
often used the following paradigm over the years: Subjects are presented with a
fictional report about a fire in a department store.  Flammable materials, such
as improperly stored gas canisters and oil paints, are said to have caused the
fire. Some subjects are then presented with a retraction of the report, stating
that no flammable materials were stored in the department store. In the final
comprehension test, many participants, despite the retraction, cite the
flammable materials as the cause of the fire ([Lewandowsky et al.,
2012](https://doi.org/10.1177/1529100612451018)).  A theoretical explanation
for this frequently replicated experimental finding is the assumption that
people construct mental models of unfolding events ([Lewandowsky et al.,
2012](https://doi.org/10.1177/1529100612451018); [Wilkes & Leatherbarrow,
1988](https://doi.org/10.1080/02724988843000168)). In doing so, a mental
network model is created from various factors that influence one another. If,
in hindsight, one factor turns out to be incorrect, a gap arises, and the
network collapses.  Thus, the retraction of the report is ignored so that the
mental model can remain intact.  This article examines the influence of
uncertain alternative information on retracted news reports. Using an
experiment, we aim to investigate whether the Continued Influence Effect is
greater when the retraction of misinformation disseminated by the media is
presented alongside an uncertain alternative (W<sub>u</sub>) or when the
retraction is presented alongside a certain (W<sub>s</sub>) or no alternative
information (W<sub>0</sub>).  In the first step, we aim to replicate existing
research findings by [Johnson and Seifert
(1994)](https://doi.org/10.1037/0278-7393.20.6.1420) and therefore predict that
the effect can be reduced more effectively by a retraction accompanied by a
certain alternative than by one with no alternative. The inference value of the
W<sub>0</sub> condition will thus be higher than in the W<sub>s</sub>
condition.  Next, we will examine the influence of an uncertain information
alternative on the inference value and relate it to other types of retraction.
In doing so, we will compare W<sub>0</sub> with W<sub>u</sub>, assuming that
the inference value of W<sub>0</sub> will be higher. In the five comparison of
W<sub>s</sub> with W<sub>u</sub>, we predict that W<sub>u</sub> will exhibit a
higher inference value. Because the experimental conditions should differ only
in terms of the inference value and not in general memory performance, we
assume that there will be no differences in factual recall performance between
the conditions.  Based on the definition of the Continued Influence Effect, we
predict that for participants who noticed the retraction and correction, there
is a negative correlation between the inference value and the reported
credibility of the correction message.


## Methods

### Participants 

Approximately 100 students each recruited eight acquaintances as participants and
conducted at least four in-person experiments and no more than four online experiments.
Participants who were at least 18 years old, had normal vision, and spoke German at
native-speaker proficiency met the eligibility criteria for the experiment. Of the
total 753 participants aged 18 to 86 (*M* = 29.50, *SD* = 14.61, *Mdn* = 22) who took part in the experiment, 301 were male, 439 were female, and 5 identified as non-binary. They
were divided into three equal-sized groups of 246, 253, and 254 participants.
Their participation in the experiment was voluntary, and ethical guidelines were strictly
followed. Only the data of those participants who completed the experiment
and gave consent to the processing of their data were analyzed.
Calculations from the pre-registration indicated a sample size of 690
and a group size of 230 participants each to achieve a power of more than 80% in the planned Welch tests with α = .05. To determine the minimum relevant effect, the following values from previous experiments were used: W<sub>0</sub> (*M* = 0.95, *SD*
= 1.42), W<sub>u</sub> (*M* = 0.615, *SD* = 1.085), W<sub>s</sub> (*M* = 0.28, *SD* = 0.75)


## Materials

The experiment was conducted on a computer using a Chrome-based web browser
such as Firefox or Google Chrome. A keyboard with a German layout (QWERTZ) and
a mouse were used. The participant sat in front of the monitor, which displayed
the experiment website in full screen.  SoSci Survey was used to present the
news article titled “Düsseldorf Drug Dealer Found Dead” and the accompanying
questions to the participants step by step. Depending on the experimental
conditions, the article is approximately 200 words long and deals with the
police investigation of a case in which a drug dealer was found dead in his
apartment.  Although it was initially reported that the methamphetamine dealer
had been violently killed during a drug deal gone wrong, a thorough police
investigation revealed that this was not the case. The article can be found in
the appendix.


## Procedure

First, the participant carefully read through the informational text in the
introduction.  After the consent form was digitally signed, a questionnaire
collected demographic data such as age and gender. Then the experiment began
with a click on “Next.” The newspaper article “Düsseldorf Drug Dealer Found
Dead” appeared sentence by sentence on the screen. Clicking the “Next” button
with the mouse displayed the next sentence. In the fourth sentence, a piece of
misinformation was presented. The ninth sentence was different for each
experimental group. Either the misinformation from the fourth sentence was
retracted, or it was retracted and a certain or uncertain alternative piece of
information was provided.  After sentence nine, a Tetris game appeared on the
screen, which was played for 10 minutes.  Subsequently, the following types of
questions were asked of the test subject.

- Five inference questions with open-ended response options.
- Five fact questions with a choice of four options.
- A single yes/no question asking whether the correction of the misinformation
  was noticed.
- A slider with one hundred levels representing the credibility of the
  correction.

After the experiment, the participant was provided with an informative text
explaining the subject of the experiment. In the final step, participants were
given the option to consent to sharing the collected data with the
experimenter. The total duration of the experiment is approximately 20 minutes.


### Research Design

The experiment followed a between-subjects design, in which participants were
randomly assigned by SoSci Survey to one of three experimental conditions.  The
variation in the ninth sentence, which differed depending on the experimental
condition, constituted the independent variable. The dependent variable was
measured by the participants’ responses to the questions. Thus, based on the
performance on the inference questions, an inference score was calculated that
represents the recall of the misinformation. The fact recall score reflects
recall performance, which was derived from the performance on the five fact
questions.

## Discussion

In our experiment, we examined the effect of different types of retraction on
the Continued Influence Effect and tested the influence of W<sub>u</sub>,
W<sub>s</sub>, and W<sub>0</sub> on the inference score and fact recall score.
The average inference score was significantly higher in the W<sub>0</sub>
condition than in the W<sub>s</sub> condition. This means that in the case of a
retraction without an alternative piece of information, more inferences were
drawn from the false information. Thus, the effectiveness of a retraction
increases when a reliable alternative piece of information is also presented.
This finding corresponds to the Continued Influence Effect that [Johnson and
Seifert (1994)](https://doi.org/10.1037/0278- 7393.20.6.1420) identified in
their pioneering study.  Because the differences in inference scores between
W<sub>0</sub> and W<sub>s</sub> and between W<sub>s</sub> and W<sub>u</sub>
were not significant despite high statistical power, no substantive conclusions
can be drawn. It cannot be clearly stated whether a retraction with an
uncertain alternative is more or less effective than a retraction with a
certain alternative or no retraction at all.  Furthermore, we observed similar
recall performance across the three groups, which implies that the formation of
inferences from false information is influenced by the type of retraction.  In
addition, we also found a slight negative correlation between the perceived
credibility of the correction and a person’s inference value, provided the
person recalled the correction. People who made many inferences draw more
inferences from the misinformation consider the correction of that information
to be less credible than people who draw few inferences from the
misinformation.


### Implications

Our findings vividly illustrate the challenges involved in dealing with
misinformation and can be used to inform the public more effectively and to
reduce the negative impact of misinformation on society. For example,
[Lewandowsky et al. (2012)](https://doi.org/10.1177/1529100612451018) found
that corrections to misinformation should emphasize the correct facts, be
concise, and ideally be presented with supporting graphics.  Similarly, the
findings help us understand fundamental cognitive processes and can be
explained by the mental models proposed by [Johnson and Seifert
(1994)](https://doi.org/10.1037/0278-7393.20.6.1420) . The retraction creates a
gap in the model, making it difficult to understand the event without the
retracted misinformation. While a reliable alternative piece of information
allows for model adjustment, a retraction alone leads people to rely on the old
mental model despite the correction and to ignore the correction


## Limitations

In order to recruit participants quickly, each participant in the internship
conducted the experiment with friends and acquaintances, partly in person and
partly online.  This led to various sources of interference, such as noise or
differences in computer hardware. The results were also influenced by the
demographics of the participants, who, due to the selection of the sample, were
predominantly from academic families with a high level of education. It was
certainly also confusing that the newspaper article was presented sentence by
sentence.  While this ensured that the participants read every line of the text
carefully, it would have been more realistic if the newspaper article had been
presented as a whole.  Finally, it can be noted that the experiment’s user
interface bore little resemblance to a newspaper article or an online forum.


## Future Research

To address this shortcoming, future studies could replicate the user interfaces
of well-known internet forums and online media to examine the influence of
information presentation. [Vosoughi et
al.](2018)(https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aap955) found that misinformation
spreads online six times faster than truthful content and that 70% of users
could not distinguish between true and false news.  Against the backdrop of new
technologies that enable the rapid creation and dissemination of content on
social media, this field of research is likely to gain increasing relevance in
the future.
