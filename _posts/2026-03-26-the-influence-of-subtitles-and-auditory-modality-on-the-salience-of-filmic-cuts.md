Although subtitles are everywhere in modern digital media and movies, their
influence on the cognitive processing of cinematic cuts remains understudied.
In this experiment we investigated how subtitles and modality influence the
salience of filmic cuts. Our 25 participants completed a cut detection task
with 180 clips containing zero, one or two within-scene cuts. The
within-participant design was balanced in four conditions. We found that
subtitles significantly reduce both the Cut Detection Performance and in-
crease Cut Detection Reaction Times. Furthermore, while the presence of audio
reduces Cut Detection Performance, it did not significantly influence
response times. Although we hypothesized an interaction effect, the results
suggest that the cognitive load is additive for both factors. We discuss the
findings with the frameworks of the Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning.
We conclude that filmmakers must account for these effects when placing
critical visual information in subtitled content to avoid overtaxing viewer’s
limited cognitive resources. Notably, post-hoc analysis revealed significantly
slower detection for second cuts compared to first cuts. Future research
should investigate these effects across diverse scene types and continue to
extend the paradigm of [Papenmeyer et al. (2024)](10.31234/osf.io/n4z6y) by
incorporating higher frequencies of cinematic cuts per clip.



Nowadays, the average German person spends over six and a half hours every day
consuming media, half of which is video based and less than 15% being text
based ([ARD/ZDF-Forschungskommission,
2024](https://www.ard-zdf-medienforschung.de/mediennutzung/ard-zdf-
medienstudie/)). Whereas previous generations relied on literature to
stimulate mental imagery, contemporary audiences favour increasingly
faster-cut, darker and movement-rich video content ([Cutting et al.,
2011](https://doi.org/10.1068/i0441aap)). However, a small relic of our
literary past persists within this digital landscape: subtitles. They serve as
a bridge between our past as readers and our present as viewers, allowing us
to read what characters are saying in the same moment we are watching their
stories unfold on the screen. This real-time text overlay allows us to watch
movies from other languages, or keep watching, when there is no sound
available. Beyond convenience, subtitles are important for the accessibility
of filmic content for the Deaf and hard-of-hearing community and serve as a
vital pedagogical tool for language learners. But even though subtitles are in
everyday usage and most of the video content on social media comes with
automatically generated subtitles included, we still know very little about
the effects subtitles have on viewer’s attention and whether they interfere
with any other continuity editing techniques that filmmakers use to ensure
coherent storytelling.



To understand how attention works in the context of multimedia input, we must
build on Richard Mayer’s Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning
([CTML](https://learning-theories.com/cognitive-theory-of-multimedia-learning-mayer.html)).
Mayer posits that humans process information through two distinct channels:
the verbal channel which specifically processes sounds and spoken words and
the pictorial channel which only processes images and written text. On the one
hand subtitles create a unique cognitive challenge because they are written
text and must be processed by the pictorial channel, where they compete for
limited resources with the actual filmic images. This competition increases
cognitive load, especially when a viewer must simultaneously integrate visual
scenes, on-screen text, and often a musical soundtrack or dialogue. On the
other hand, movies without sound relief cognitive load, because less
information must be decoded in the verbal channel.



In line with this theoretical background, many experiments found edit
blindness, which is the phenomenon that even when explicitly instructed to
detect cuts by pressing a button, participants still fail to identify roughly
15 % of them ([Smith & Henderson, 2008](https://doi.org/10.16910/jemr.2.2.6)).
Furthermore, Smith and [Martin-Portugues Santacreu
(2017)](10.1080/15213269.2016.1160789) found that audio leads to greater edit
blindness when presenting match-action cuts. Edit blindness mostly disappeared
in con- ditions without audio, which demonstrates the significant role that
audio plays in directing viewers’ attention. While previous studies, such as
[Papenmeier et al. (2024)](https://doi.org/10.1037/aca0000476), have explored
how viewing modes (3D vs. 2D) affect cut salience in combination with
different modalities (visual, audiovisual), there is a significant research
gap regarding how subtitles interact with these processes. Both [Papenmeier et
al. (2024)](https://doi.org/10.1037/aca0000476) and [Smith and Henderson
(2008)](10.1080/15213269.2016.1160789)
found that cuts are easier to detect if auditory information is removed. But
could this effect be mediated by the presentation of subtitles?

The present study addresses this gap by investigating the impact of both modality and
subtitles on the salience of filmic cuts. Utilizing a cut detection paradigm, participants will
view 180 video clips and be instructed to press the spacebar as quickly as possible upon de-
tecting a cut. Based on the methodology of Papenmeier et al. (2024), we will analyse both the
Cut Detection Performance and the Cut Detection Reponse Time and will employ a similar
two by two within-subjects design to answer the question: To what extent do auditory sound-
tracks and visual subtitles independently and interactively influence the salience of filmic
cuts? By exploring these variables, this study aims to provide practical relevance for media
design and a deeper understanding of the modern viewer's experience.

First, based on prior research and the CTML, the inclusion of audio will significantly
reduce cut detection performance and increase the reaction times compared to purely visual
representations. Therefore, we expect a main effect of modality. Second, because of higher
cognitive load, the presence of subtitles will result in lower cut detection rates and longer re-
action times compared to presentations without subtitles. Therefore, we expect a main effect
of subtitles. Third, there will be a significant interaction between modality and the presence
of subtitles, such that subtitles will more sharply decrease cut detection performance when
audio is absent than when audio is present, because the viewer will try to get the missing au-
ditory information from the visual / pictorial channel and therefore have increased load on
that route

# Methods

## Participants

Each of the three experimenters recruited naïve participants through their friends and
family. A total of 25 participants (13 men, 12 women) performed the experiment between
March 28, 2026, and April 5, 2026. The age of participants ranged from 18 to 64 years (M =
38.85, SD = 18.19, Mdn = 22.5). All participants were native German speakers over the age
of 18 and reported normal or corrected-to-normal vision. The sample was characterized by a
high educational background and high socio-economic status, due to being close to a univer-
sity setting. Additionally, participants reported high media consumption, with over 50% con-
suming more than five hours of movie content per week, and half regularly using subtitles
when watching films (see Table 1 in Appendix A).

We followed strict ethical guidelines, and all participants provided their voluntary in-
formed consent. While our preregistration specified an exclusion threshold of 80% for atten-
tion check accuracy, a post-hoc analysis of the check items revealed that 14 of the 180 checks
(8 %) were ambiguous with an item comprehension rate of under 10% and failed to measure
attention accurately. To account for this poor item reliability, we adjusted our overall exclu-
sion criterion to 70%, which prevented the unwarranted exclusion of otherwise high-quality
data. A target sample size of N = 36 participants was preregistered to achieve a power of at
least .80 (with α = .05) for detecting a small to medium effect size. Although our final sam-
ple did not reach this preregistered goal, we oriented our design toward the methodology of
Papenmeier et al. (2024), who utilized 24 participants. It should be noted, however, that our
design utilized 180 trials per participant compared to their 480


## Apparatus

The experiment was programmed using PsychoPy (Version 2025.2.3, Peirce et al.
(2019)) and the pre- and post-experiment surveys were hosted online via Pavlovia. Partici-
pants completed the experiment individually in a quiet room, seated in front of a 16-inch lap-
top provided by the experimenters. Prior to the main experiment, participants completed prac-
tice trials during which they adjusted the laptop’s speaker’s volume to a comfortable listening
level.


## Stimuli

The experimental stimuli consisted of 180 video clips, each ranging from 8 to 12 sec-
onds in duration. These clips were sourced from eight different films1 within the "Comedy
and Family" category of the German streaming service ARD Mediathek. To ensure appropri-
ate experimental control, within scene cuts were distributed evenly across the source material


in such a way that 70 clips featured zero cuts, 70 clips featured one cut, and 40 clips featured
two cuts. Several constraints were applied to the stimulus generation. First, to maintain narra-
tive continuity, all edits consisted strictly of within-scene cuts, though the overall visual con-
text varied broadly across the 180 trials. Second, because the experimental design required
the presentation of subtitles, all selected target clips featured at least one visible character and
contained active spoken dialogue. And third, no clips followed each other in direct succession
in the original film



## Research question

The study employed a within-subjects experimental design. The two independent var-
iables were subtitle presence (subtitles vs. no subtitles) and audio modality (visual vs. audio-
visual). The crossing of these factors resulted in four distinct experimental conditions, to
which all participants were exposed in a within-participants design. The experiment consisted
of 180 total trials per participant, distributed equally such that each of the four conditions
contained 45 video clips. Within the set of 180 trials, the 70 clips that lacked any cuts served
as filler trials to hide the primary objective of the task. To prevent sequence and stimulus-spe-
cific confounding effects, the presentation order of the 180 trials was fully randomized for
each participant. Furthermore, the assignment of specific video clips to the four experimental
conditions was counterbalanced across the sample. This ensured that every video clip ap-
peared equally often in each condition across all participants, while guaranteeing that no sin-
gle participant viewed the same clip more than once. Finally, to ensure sustained participant
engagement, 30 attention checks were randomly presented throughout the trial sequence.


## Procedure

The entire experimental session lasted approximately 40 minutes. First, participants
were provided with general information about the study, gave their informed consent, and
completed a brief demographic questionnaire. Then, the experiment started with a practice

block of 12 trials, which included four attention checks. During this phase, participants re-
ceived immediate feedback on their performance to ensure they understood that their reaction
times were being actively measured. Following the practice block, participants were given the
opportunity to ask the experimenter any clarifying questions. Once all questions were an-
swered, the experimenter left the room, and the main experimental phase began.
Each trial followed the same structure: a 500 ms blank screen, followed by a 200 ms
central fixation cross to anchor visual attention, and finally a video clip from one of the four
experimental conditions. Participants were instructed to press the spacebar as quickly as pos-
sible when detecting a cinematic cut. To ensure sustained engagement throughout the task, 30
randomized attention checks were presented across the 180 trials, appearing immediately af-
ter a clip. To mitigate fatigue, the experiment included two-minute breaks after the 59th and
119th trials. Upon completing the session, participants filled out a questionnaire to report
their prior familiarity with the eight source films



# Results


Prior to the main analysis the data were screened to prevent extreme reaction times or
guessing behaviour. Following our preregistered criteria, target trials with reaction times be-
low 200 ms or above 1500 ms were excluded from the final dataset. Overall, familiarity with
the stimulus material was low, because participants recognized only one film on average
(Range = 1 - 5). The film Monsieur Claude und seine Töchter was the most recognized,
with approximately half of the sample reporting prior exposure to it. Finally, a post-experi-
ment control check confirmed that the visual task requirements were met, with 100% of par-
ticipants self-reporting that they were able to read the on-screen subtitles without any diffi-
culty

# Discussion


The present study investigated the influence of modality and subtitles on Cut Detec-
tion Performance and reaction times. While our hypotheses predicted that both audio and sub-
titles would impair performance and potentially interact, the results present a more nuanced
picture of how human attention bottlenecks when processing multimedia.
Partially supporting the first hypothesis, the presence of audio reduced Cut Detection
Performance but did not slow down physical reaction times (p = .700). This does align with
CTML’s Dual-Channel Assumption that sound is processed independently in the verbal chan-
nel and, because it does not directly compete for the foveal vision to physically see a cut, it
does not delay the motoric reaction time to a cut once it is visually noticed. Even through it is
only partially replicating the results from Papenmeier et al. (2024), the fact that sound low-
ered overall accuracy suggests an overload at the Working Memory level (Mayer & Moreno,
2005)


As predicted in the second hypothesis, subtitles significantly reduced cut detection ac-
curacy and slowed down response times. This outcome is a demonstration of the Split-Atten-
tion Principle and the Limited Capacity Assumption (Mayer & Moreno, 2005). According to
CTML, both the cinematic cuts as a visual narrative and the subtitles as the text must be pro-
cessed through the single pictorial channel. Because this channel has a strictly limited capac-
ity, the viewer is forced to split their visual attention. To read the subtitles, viewers must fix-
ate their foveal vision on the text at the bottom of the screen, pulling their focal attention
away from the center of the frame where visual continuity is typically monitored. Because the
brain must actively disengage from semantic text processing to register a cut, this explains
both the reduced detection rate and the delayed reaction times.
Contrary to our third hypothesis, there was no significant interaction between modal-
ity and subtitles for either performance or reaction time. The factors impaired detection inde-
pendently. However, the lack of an interaction suggests that human reading behavior in the
presence of subtitles is highly automatic and that the resulting cognitive loads are additive
and not multiplicative. This also explains the similar results and the missing interaction Pa-
penmeier et al. (2024) found in their experiment. An explanation could be that the verbal
channel and pictorial channel have separate capacity limits and overloading one doesn't turn
off the other. Sound could load general working memory, while subtitles specifically bottle-
neck the visual processing channel. Therefore, they act as two independent influences to the
viewer's cognitive load, resulting in a parallel effect pattern rather than an interaction



Participants were significantly slower to detect the second cut compared to the first
cut. We only hypothesized about the qualitative difference of first and second cuts in hind-
sight and expected the second reaction time to be shorter, because of a higher general atten-
tion level, when a cut was detected. Because we found the reverse, an underlying process could be a Working Memory Overflow, when a second cut happens shortly after a first cut.
This also could explain the wider distribution of the reaction times seen in Figure 2.
Based on our analysis, media professionals should avoid placing critical visual clues
right after a cut when subtitles are present. Furthermore, editors can effectively mask tech-
nical cuts by employing sound bridges, which utilize auditory load to increase edit blindness
and ensure a seamless narrative flow. Since auditory and textual information impose an addi-
tive cognitive load, it is essential to simplify one modality when the other is mandatory to
protect the viewer’s limited working memory capacity.





## Limitations


Although the total volume of experimental trials approached half of those utilized by
Papenmeier et al. (2024), a higher volume of trials would likely have increased the statistical
power and reduced individual variance. However, the experimental duration was intentionally
capped at approximately 40 minutes to decrease participant fatigue and minimize attrition, a
necessary consideration given that participants received no monetary compensation.
Furthermore, we aimed at a very diverse set of clips. Future research should consider
pre-categorizing stimuli to determine whether the narrative content of a clip exerts an inde-
pendent effect on cut detection rates
