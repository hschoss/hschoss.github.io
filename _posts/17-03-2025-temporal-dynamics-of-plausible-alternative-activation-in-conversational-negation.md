Negation is a fundamental linguistic phenomenon that has been researched
philosophically in the past and psychologically in recent decades. To
understand how negation affects the activation level of plausible alternatives,
we studied how different prime target relations influence response time in a
lexical decision task with and without delay. We expected an interaction effect
between delay and prime target relation by comparing the response time in the
unrelated condition with the negated entity, good alternative, and worse
alternative with and without delay. Furthermore, the same kind of interaction
was expected for the good versus worse alternative. In a within-participants
design with a sample of 313 participants, no interaction effects were found
between the prime target relation and delay. All four hypotheses were
falsified. Unexpectedly, we found three significant main effects of prime
target relation. Although our experiment did not reach a power of 80%, the
symmetry and consistency of our results indicate that we have worked in a
methodically correct manner.  Therefore, our results can be interpreted with an
adaption of the mental model theory. Future research could explore the role of
different lengths of delay and come up with an improved version of an attention
check for the participants.


Negation is a fundamental component of human language and has been studied in
multiple disciplines for centuries. Aristotle was one of the first to study
negation. In his pivotal work [Analytica
priora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prior_Analytics) he examined negation
from a logical perspective as a truth- functional operator. He showed that a
statement and its negation cannot both be true simultaneously and distinguished
between affirmative (1.) and negative (2.) statements ([Ebert & Nortmann,
2007](https://books.google.de/books/about/Analytica_priora_Buch_I.html?id=idcGzwEACAAJ&redir_esc=y))

1. There is a whale.
2. There is not a whale.

With the advent of computer technology, the academic discussion of negation can
no longer be restricted to only philosophical, logical, and linguistic
disciplines, but must also involve psychological experiments. An early
psychological discovery from the 1960s and 1970s is the interaction between
affirmative or negative polarity and the truth values that propositions
express. Research has shown that true affirmative sentences are easier to
evaluate than false affirmative sentences, whereas the opposite is true for
negative sentences: true negative sentences are more difficult to process than
false ones ([Kaup et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.4324/9780203936443))
. [Kaup et al. (2005)](10.1016/j.pragma.2005.09.012) replicated this effect in a
study, where they presented pictures alongside sentences, half of which matched
the situation while the other half did not. As predicted, they found the
negation by truth value interaction as an early effect and a main effect of
truth value with a delay of 1500 ms


Individuals confuse the polarity of a given sentence with its falsity because
conversational negation behaves differently than logical negation, which often
lacks context.  [Kruszewski et al.
(2016)](https://doi.org/10.1162/COLI_a_00262) use the distributional semantics
approach to show the pragmatic side of negation. They derive vector-based
meaning representations of words by generalizing the 4 contexts in which those
words occur in large text corpora. These representations can model lexical
aspects of meaning like word analogy or synonymy but struggle with semantic
properties like negation. If sentence (1.) denotes the set of all whales, then
(2.) denotes the complement of the set. If we interpret a negation of a
predicate as solely the complement of the corresponding set, then (3.) and (4.)
should be equally plausible

3. This is not a whale … it is a dolphin.
4. This is not a whale … it is a washing machine.

But sentence (3.) is much more likely to be uttered in a natural conversation
than sentence (4.) ([Capuano et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.1111/cogs.13015).
In contrast to Aristotle’s view of negation as a static distributional
representation of a single statement, [Kruszewski et al.
(2016)](https://doi.org/10.1162/COLI_a_00262) think of it as a function that,
given the negated predicate, produces a probability distribution over plausible
alternatives.

[Khemlani et al. (2012)](https://doi.org/10.1080/20445911.2012.660913) propose
a theory of negation rooted in the theory of mental models and relying on two
intertwined processes that occur as one understands a proposition.  Firstly,
the intensions are composed of the meanings of their words and grammatical
relations while being influenced by knowledge of the individual. Secondly, the
intensions are used to build or update a mental model of the situation
described. Based on another person’s remarks, individuals generate mental
models for each possible situation. The generation is based on a principle of
truth because mental models represent what is true but not what is false,
unless an assertion explicitly depends on what is false. The idea that negation
is the process of juxtaposing alternative worlds or mental models can be found
in [Repp and Spalek (2021)](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcomm.2021.682009).

This experiment will investigate what concepts are activated and inhibited
automatically over time, when the negation of an entity is processed. Based on
the German experiment of [Capuano et al.
(2023)](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13421-023-01434-2) affirmative and negative
prime sentences were presented 5 via audio followed by a lexical decision task.
To study the temporal characteristics of the representational process of
negation in mental models, we used trials with and without a 750 ms delay. We
analyzed the response time for correct responses in trials where a negated
sentence was presented as a prime. The prime target relation was either a
negated entity, good alternative, worse alternative or unrelated alternative.

Firstly, the activation of the negated entity decreases over time, because
based on the idea of the principle of truth, updating a mental model
representing something that is not there, increases cognitive load over time.
Therefore, we expect an interaction between prime target relation (negated
entity, unrelated) and delay (0 ms, 750 ms). Secondly, the activation for good
alternatives increases over time, because based on [Kruszewski et al.
(2016)](https://doi.org/10.1162/COLI_a_00262) we believe that plausible
alternatives are automatically activated when a word is heard. That is why, we
expect an interaction between prime target relation (good alternative,
unrelated) and delay (0 ms, 750 ms). Thirdly, as per literature the activation
for good alternatives increases more than for worse alternatives over time
([Capuano et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13421-023-01434-2); [Kaup et
al., 2007] (https://doi.org/10.4324/9780203936443)). That is, we expect an
interaction between prime target relation (good alternative, worse alternative)
and delay (0 ms, 750 ms). Fourth, the activation for worse alternatives
decreases over time.  That is, we expect an interaction between prime target
relation (worse alternative, unrelated) and delay (0 ms, 750 ms)


## Method

### Participants

Each of the 47 students recruited between nine and 11 naïve participants in a
timeframe of four weeks to achieve a sample size of 423 to 517 subjects. A
sample size of 360 participants was required in order to achieve a power of at
least 80% with α = .05 for detecting a mean interaction of 30 ms with the
planned repeated measures ANOVAs. The calculation was based on a previous
version of the experiment, which provided the following standard deviations: p
(*SD* = 230), pa (*SD* = 50), and pb (*SD* = 50).  Of the total of 355 participants
313 were included in the analysis aged between 18 and 80 years (*M* = 29.9, *SD* =
14.61, *Mdn* = 22) who took part in the experiment. Out of those participants 121
were male, 188 female, and 4 non-binary. We analyzed only the data of
participants who provided informed consent, completed the experiment, and were
at least 18 years old. Additionally, we excluded seven participants who
reported a native language other than German and three participants who had
previously participated in or seen a very similar experiment. We followed
strict ethical guidelines and all participants took part voluntarily.  Although
the survey period of the experiment was prolonged, the power was too low.


### Apparatus

The experiment was conducted on a computer with loudspeakers, mouse and
keyboard in German layout (QWERTZ) using a Chrome-based internet browser. The
test subject sat in front of the monitor on which the test website SoSciSurvey
was displayed. The experiment was hosted with Pavlovia and programmed using
PsychoPy, a Python library specially developed for creating and conducting
psychological experiment.  The students of the course participated in a cloze
task prior to the experiment and completed sentences such as *This is not a
whale, it’s ____*. We picked German nouns (e.g.  whale), checked our good
alternatives for bidirectionality and made sure that none of our worse
alternatives were in the results of the cloze task.

### Research Design

The four types of relation between the noun in the prime sentence and the
target word (negated entity, good alternative, worse alternative, unrelated)
combined with the two delays (0 ms, 750 ms) resulted in eight experimental
conditions, which all participants were exposed to in an
within-participants-design. In addition to the experimental trials involving 64
negated and 32 affirmative sentences, 64 filler trials were included. These
filler trials consisted of negated sentences with non-words in the lexical
decision task, as well as affirmative sentences with both words and non-words.
However, filler trials were excluded from the analysis of the dependent
variable, which was the response time in the lexical decision task.  On top of
that, 40 attention checks were added to make sure the participants were engaged


### Procedure

The experiment started in SoSci Survey and it was explained that the next steps
would be conducted via the scientific hosting platform Pavlovia. At the
beginning the demographic data of age, gender, and native language were
collected. Prior to the experiment, participants were informed about the
lexical decision task, data storage, duration, and cancellation option and gave
consent to the analysis of their data. To ensure proper processing of the
experiment, a volume test was conducted before its commencement. In this test,
numbers were read aloud by a computer-generated voice through the loudspeakers,
and participants were asked to press the corresponding number. This procedure
was designed to confirm that participants had adjusted the volume settings
correctly. After that, informational slides about the experimental procedure
were displayed.

A sentence of the form *There is [a/an]/[not a/not an] X* (German *Dort ist
[ein/e]/[kein/e] X*) was played via audio. After a delay of either 0 ms or 750
ms, a string of letters was presented, which could either be a word or a
non-word. Participants had to judge as fast and accurately as possible whether
the presented letter sequence was a word (“j”) or a non-word (“n”) by pressing
the corresponding keys (lexical decision task).

Before the actual data collection began, participants were required to complete
10 practice trials and were reminded to respond as quickly and accurately as
possible.

Throughout the experiment, feedback was provided for incorrect answers in the
form of a 2000ms delay while displaying “false”. Sometimes the audio stopped
mid-sentence and participants had to klick on the correct sentence There is
[a/an]/[not a/not an] they just listened to (attention check task). Finally,
after completing the 10 practice trials, 160 experimental trials, and 40
attention checks, participants were asked whether they had participated in an
experiment like this before and were thanked for their participation.


## Results

To ensure that participants were engaged in our experiment, we established
performance-related criteria like an attention check and benchmarks for the
lexical decision task. Eight participants did not satisfy the minimum of 80%
correct answers on the attention checks. In the experimental and filler trial
three participants had a mean performance worse than 80% correct in the lexical
decision task and were therefore excluded. For the analysis of our dependent
variable, the response time, we only included data from the experimental
trials.  Moreover, we removed all trials with wrong responses and trials with
response times lower than 250 ms or higher than 6000 ms. Any participant with
less than four trials left in one of the eight conditions after the
aforementioned exclusion was completely removed from the data set (*n* = 1
participant excluded). In total, 6.57% of the initial participants datasets
were excluded


### Statistical Analysis

The data were analyzed with four two-factor repeated measures ANOVAs with the
dependent measure response time in the lexical decision task and the factors delay (0 ms, 750
ms; within) and different prime target relations (within), which depended on the hypothesis

As shown in Figure 1, no interaction effect was found between delay (0 ms, 750

relation (good alternative, unrelated) and delay (0 ms, 750 ms),

relation was again found, *F*(1, 312) = 7.84, *p* < .01. No significant effects
were found in the analysis of the third hypothesis: There was no interaction
between prime target relation (good alternative, worse alternative) and delay
(0 ms, 750 ms), *F*(1, 312) = 0.01, *p* = .938. Similarly, no interaction was
found between prime target relation (worse alternatives, unrelated) and delay
(0ms, 750 ms), *F*(1, 312) = 0.12, p = .73. However, a significant main effect
of prime target relation was observed, *F*(1, 312) = 7.39, *p* < .01


## Disucssion

In our experiment, we examined the influence of negation processing on the
activation levels of plausible alternatives over time using a lexical decision
task. A within-participants- design incorporating experimental trials, filler
trials, and attention check trials was used to measure response times in eight
conditions. We interpreted the response time as an indicator of the activation
level of mental concepts, whereas high response times imply low activation
levels and vice versa.

All our hypotheses were falsified, because no interaction effect was found
between prime target relation and delay. Unexpectedly, we found three
significant main effects of prime target relation when comparing the unrelated
condition with the negated entity, good alternative and worse alternative.
Figure 1 illustrates this main effect and shows a decrease in response time
when the delay was set to 750 ms compared to no delay. Therefore, our results
suggest that when processing negation all targets are equally strong
automatically activated over time.



A possible theoretical interpretation of our results is that the 750 ms delay
enables the generation of mental models for more possibilities, thereby
enhancing mental activation of related concepts across all prime target
relations (Khemlani et al., 2012; Repp & Spalek, 2021). Supporting this
interpretation, the mean response time was higher for the unrelated condition
compared to the negated entity condition, suggesting lower activation levels.
However, there should be no main effect of prime target relation when comparing
unrelated alternatives to good alternatives, as unrelated alternatives do not
share conceptual connections with the prime word. This was contradicted by the
response times not being similar between the 0 ms and 750 ms delay conditions
for unrelated alternatives.  Furthermore, there should be a smaller main effect
of prime target relation for implausible alternatives compared to plausible
alternatives. However, response times over time remained parallel for good and
worse alternatives. Both contradictions weaken this interpretation.  An
alternative explanation is that the delay may instead allow for general
response preparation, regardless of the specific prime target relation
presented. Based on our analysis, both explanations rely on the assumption that
no interaction exists between prime target relation and delay

### Limitations

Each student enrolled in the Expra 2 course was responsible for recruiting
participants. As a result, the sample was highly selected, with almost 60% of
the participants being in their 20s or 50s, WEIRD and having an academic
background. The average response time in the experimental trials we analyzed
differed by approximately 130 ms between these two age groups, hinting age
related effects on response time. This is especially concerning given that our
power analysis estimated the interaction effect to be 30 ms.  Furthermore, our
research design was complex and included approximately 200 trials per
participant, making it quite extensive. This could have led to position effects
or fatigue effects. The biggest limitation is our lack of statistical power.
Although, the data collection period was extended, we reached only 87 % of our
planned minimum sample size. As a result, our findings have limited
generalizability, and we must interpret the effects with caution.


### Implications

Nevertheless, it can be assumed that the experiment was constructed and
conducted methodically correct, because our results were highly consistent and
parallel. Therefore, despite the lack of power, the interpretation remains
meaningful. Given that negation is a fundamental aspect of human language, our
findings are important as they can be applied to improve communication
strategies and reduce cognitive load in various fields. For example, in the
advertising industry, the communication of a message could be facilitated by
priming individuals with plausible alternative words and incorporating a small
delay of 750 ms, which would increase the activation levels of related
concepts. Additionally, when communicating complex and negated information
about sensitive topics through media like radio or TV, our results could help
to reduce cognitive load and improve understanding


### Future research

Although we enhanced the attention check tasks compared to Capuano et al.
(2023), future research could incorporate eye-tracking technology to ensure
participant engagement and maintain the experiment's flow without requiring
frequent switching between the “n” and “f” keys and the mouse. Additionally, it
would be interesting to examine varying delay durations to assess the temporal
impact on plausible alternatives in negation. For instance, a 1500 ms delay, as
used in Kaup et al. (2005) could be implemented.
